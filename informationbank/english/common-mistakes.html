Common Mistakes



* had visited or have visited or visited ?
I had visited my grand father last year.
I visited my grand father last year.
Ans:
I visited..." is correct because you are stating a fact.
You need to use the perfect tenses when talking about a past event which continues to the time being described.
Examples:
(1) He did many things last year: he graduated from college, he toured Japan, and he visited his grandfather.
(2) Tom's grandfather died last year. Tom had visited him three months before he died. (Tom visited him in February; his grandfather died in May.)

* If a(the) specific file was uploaded, then which sentence below is correct?
The file had been uploaded
The file has been uploaded
Ans:
If you want to use 'had been uploaded' (past perfect) you would have to relate that to an earlier time as in: The film had been uploaded before we turned off the computer.
The use of 'has been uploaded' relates to now as in: The film has been uploaded and so we don't need to wait any longer.

* Me vs Myself:
Wait for me!
She likes me.

I wash myself.
I told myself it couldn't be true.
Tired of waiting, I just did it myself.
  • My friends and me went to Paris. (Incorrect)
  • My friends and I went to Paris. (Correct)
  • She came from Rome to meet I. (Incorrect)
  • She came from Rome to meet me. (Correct)

* I Vs Me: "I" comes before the verb and "me" comes after it.
Talking about multiple people (in theory)
    Luis and ___ aren't coming.
    Lori sent Jane and ___ a card last Christmas.
The easy way to figure out whether to use "I" or "me" in grammatically correct English is to take away all of the other people:
    I am not coming. » Luis and I are not coming.
    Lori sent me a card. » Lori sent Jane and me a card.
Shortcut: Using only "me"
    Me and Luis aren't coming.
    Lori sent me and Jane a card last Christmas.
Shortcut: Using only "I"
    Luis and I aren't coming.
    Lori sent Jane and I a card last Christmas.
Memorize these three sentences:
        Me and Luis aren't coming.
        Luis and I aren't coming.
        Lori sent Jane and me a card.
The first one is for casual spoken English. The second one is for formal English. The last one is good in all situations.

* get or got:
a)
Did you get the mail?
Did you got the mail?
Ans: 
Don't use "got" with "did."
Did you get the mail?
Example:
Boss: Get it?
Employee: Got it!
b) Is there any meaning difference in the below sentences ?
"Yes, I got your message."
"Yes, I did get your message."
Ans:
 No. The second one emphasises the positive.

* Did you get Vs Have you got :
'Did you get the mail?' is the past simple and would be used when you are talking about something in the past like 'yesterday' or together with another sentence like: 'When you went to the office.'
'Have you got the mail?' is the present perfect and and asks whether you have the mail with you now.

* get Vs gets Vs got
She gets (Present Simple)her books from the library every week.
Does she get (infinitive) her books every week?
She got (Past Simple) two books last week
How many books has she got (Present Perfect) this week?

*Usage of Article:
A Usage:  Before a singular noun which is countable when it is mentioned for the first time and represents no particular person or thing.
"A horse is a noble animal."
Don't use A:
    Before plural nouns.
    "Horses are noble animals."

The Usage: Before nouns of which there is only one.
"The earth is round."

 Before a noun made definite by the addition of a phrase or clause.
"The woman dressed in black."

* Went Vs Gone
"The meeting went well"
"The meeting is gone well"
Answer:
"The meeting went well" is correct.
"The meeting has gone well" if you were present at the meeting and you were talking to someone else present; even then it sounds odd without some qualification, for example "has gone well up to now". Better to stick with the simple past especially when writing feedback,
